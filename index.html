What is P.I.C?

P.I.C (or "Punctate Inner Choroidopathy" to you medical types) is described by PIC World:

"PIC is one of the diseases that are grouped together as 'White Dot syndromes' it is characterized by small yellow-white dots or lesions visible in the examination of the retina"

Who can have P.I.C?

Sadly not a lot is currently know about PIC which makes diagnosis treatment very difficult, it is currently suspected of being associated/linked with auto immune responses. What is known currently is that PIC tends to affect young, short-sighted women who will (commonly) experience:

    ** This is not an exhaustive list! **

  1. Blurred Vision (in one of both eyes)
  2. Warped Vision (Like fisheye lens)
  3. Flashing Lights
  4. Small Blinds spots in the central vision (because of the "White spots")

On top of this, between 25 - 30% of *ALL* patients develop CNV's which are fragile new blood vessels that can break leaking blood and serum, causing retinal scarring and rapid loss of visual function.

How is P.I.C treated?

One of the most commonly offered treatments for PIC is steroids (orally or via injection), the most prevalent of which is Prednisone taken orally, initially at a high dose but then tapered down over the following weeks as the side effects of prolonged Prednisone usage can include:

  1. Insomnia
  2. Weight gain
  3. Depression

It isn't just Predinsone that Ophthalmologists use in the continued fight against CNVs, another option is injections of anti VEGFs Avastin (Lucentis previously but the efficacy is low against PIC) directly into the patient's eye. Avastin works well in this case because it blocks the VEGF (which is the body's process that is responsible for growing new blood vessels) thus saving vision!. They have prove extremely effetive in managing CNVs & have gone on to be used to stablise CNVs in other retinal diseases like "wet" AMD which is the number 1 cause of permanent central vision loss

Sadly despite the treatments on offer PIC still remains a relatively unknown disease with little mainstream exposure & little over a handful of specialists worldwide. Thankfully Places like the RNIB and the Macular Society are getting more mainstream exposure which in turn is helping raise further awareness of PIC and other macular diseases.
